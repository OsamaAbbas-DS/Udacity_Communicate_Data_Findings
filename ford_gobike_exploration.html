Ford GoBike Trip Data Exploration¶

by Osama Abbas¶

Preliminary Wrangling¶

This dataset includes 183412 records of trips taken with a bike sharing service around the San Francisco Bay area during the month of February 2019. The dataset includes 16 features of these trips, including the duration, start and end timestamp, start and end station, coordinates of these stations, and some features about the users such as the birth year, gender, and the user type. In this notebook, we start with the exploration of the data in order for us to get a strong understanding of the data. Our exploration will be on three segments. first, univariate eploration, Where we determine our feaatures of interest and explore each of them individually. Second, bivariate exploration, where we look into the relationships between our features of interest. Finally, multivariate exploration, Where we look into the relationships between three or more of our features of interest.

What is the structure of your dataset?¶

There are 183412 instances in the dataset with 16 features ('duration_sec', 'start_time', 'end_time', 'start_station_id', 'start_station_name', 'start_station_latitude', 'start_station_longitude', 'end_station_id', 'end_station_name', 'end_station_latitude', 'end_station_longitude', 'bike_id', 'user_type', 'member_birth_year', 'member_gender', 'bike_share_for_all_trip').

Most variables are numeric in nature, but the variables start_station_name, end_station_name, user_type and member_gender are qualitative nominal variables.

What is/are the main feature(s) of interest in your dataset?¶

I am interested in finding the features that would best predict the duration of the trip.

What features in the dataset do you think will help support your investigation into your feature(s) of interest?¶

The features that would be expected to have an effect on the duration of the trip are the user type, member gender, time of the day and member birth year.

Discuss the distribution(s) of your variable(s) of interest. Were there any unusual points? Did you need to perform any transformations?¶

First, the user type variable. We created a bar plot of the distribution of users between customers and subscribers. As the user type variable is a qualitative nominal variable, a bar chart is the more appropriate visualization of the distribution. From the distribution we can see that 89.2% of users are subscribers, and 10.8% of users were custormers.
Secondly, regarding the gender of the members. We created a bar chart representing the gender of the members of the service. We did that by creating a new dataframe with the members gender with the missing values dropped from the new dataframe. we can see that 74.6% of the members are males, 23.3% are females, and 2.1% are other.
Thirdly, for the member birth year feature. As it is a quantitative discrete feature, the usage of a histogram to represent the proportion of members who were born within a certain range of years seems to be the appropriate choice. Our histogram is left skewed, showing a significant rise around the years of 1980 to 2000, with the peak around 1990. this would indicate that a large proportion of the members are in their 20's and 30's. It is also worth mentioning that the minimum birth year of the members is 1878, which is not possible. Therefor, this would indicate an inaccuracy in the choice of some members for their birth year.
Afterwards, we started looking at the start and end time of the trips. Both variables are quantitative discrete values, as we only extracted the hour of the day from the timestamp. the distribution of the start hour of the day is almost identical to that of the end hour of the day. Both are bimodal distributions with a rise between 7-9 in the morning and a peak around 8, and another rise between 4pm and 7pm with even a higher peak around 5pm. This would indicate that most of the members are using the service as their commute to work.
Lastly, for the duration variable, as it is a quantitative variable, a histogram is the appropraite visualisation of the distribution. The initial distribution seemed highly right skewed with no clear distribution of the trip durations. A log-type transformation seems to be the best choice in order to see the actual distribution of the duration. After transforming our variable, it was clear that the majority of the durations are between a 100 and 10000 seconds, with a peak around 400 and 900 seconds.

Of the features you investigated, were there any unusual distributions? Did you perform any operations on the data to tidy, adjust, or change the form of the data? If so, why did you do this?¶

First, with the gender feature, we had more than 8000 missing values. So we created a new dataframe without the missing values in order not to alter the original dataframe until we get a better understanding of whether the 8265 missing values for the features regarding members indicate that those records belong to users who are not members or they are missing values due to other reasons.
Afterwards, when we looked at the start and end time of the trips, the data type of theses features is an object. So we changed their data type to datetime using pandas and then extracted the hour using the datetime package in a new column and called them 'start_hour' and 'end_hour'. This allowed us to see an actual distribution of the start and end hour of the day of the trips in the dataframe.
Lastly, for the duration of the trips in seconds, the initial histogram gave us no insight of the actual distribution. Therefor we used a log-type transformation of the feature in order to give us a more detailed distribution of the duration.
